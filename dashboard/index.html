Lung Cancer Around The World

How are CO2 emissions and cigarette sales linked to lung cancer rates?


Although it is well known that smoking cigarettes does in fact cause lung cancer, we wanted to find out if CO2 emissions is also a player in this worldwide disease. The reason why we went with CO2 emissions is because it is a good indicator of all green house gasses, in hopes we could find a correlation to lung cancer rates. Our group found 3 data sets obtained from kaggle to get us started in finding this question out. We used a Lung Cancer Deaths CSV, a cigarette sales CSV, and a CO2 emissions CSV all shown in the reference tab above. To perform the ETL process, we used the Pandas Library in Python and also PostgreSQL to clean, merge, and hold our data. As we began cleaning, sorting, and studying the information, we quickly found out that we needed to bring in some other datasets to tune in our model for predicting lung cancer death rates. Some questions we found ourselves asking through the analysis were; are there more lung cancer cases in men or women? Does country GDP play a factor due to potential medical advancements? What countries have the highest emissions? How has the data changed throughout the last 20 or so years? Ultimately, we stuck to our main question of how emissions and cigarette sales are linked to lung cancer?

In the beginning of the project we were fairly certain that we would need to use an unsupervised Machine learning Model, but as we normalized and brought in other data we found that the Random Forest Machine learning Model was the best Model for our project. Overall, it was surprising to see that cigarette sales wasn't the indicator in predicting lung cancer deaths, but the country was. Again, we found ourselves wondering if medical advancements between 1st and 3rd world countries play a crucial role in both lung cancer rates and deaths. In most cases, the data does in fact show that the more cigarettes sold, the more cases recorded, and the less cigarette sales result in less cases recorded. It's hard to say for certain if CO2 emissions is directly related to lung cancer. Take the USA for example, over the last couple decades, CO2 emissions have been on the rise, but lung cancer deaths have gone down. Is this simply once again, the fact that medicine has advanced tremendously in the past 20 years?
