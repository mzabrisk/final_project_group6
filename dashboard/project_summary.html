Lung Cancer Around The World

Project Summary

The challenges and limitations

  • Defining the sssumptions
  • Normalizing data
  • Filling holes in data
  • Does data lead to predictability, pitfalls of the solution

    • Assumptions

    The assumptions are that cigarette smoking, and CO2 levels are direct factors in the risks associated with death from lung cancer.

    Normalizing data

    The primary outlier was the data for the United States.

    One of the challenges we ran into is that the areas we used to select were too large. For example in the United States the data was generalized to the entire country. Cancer rates vary from state to state. This leads to many other factors not accounted for in the specific countries.

    The data sets were small for many of the countries around the world which limited the effectiveness of the analysis, resulting in all countries not being included.

    Filling holes in data set

    The data set with the most holes in it was the GDP data set. We thought it was important to include because it may show a link between cancer rates and the wealth of a country. The countries that had missing data, had data missing at the head or tails of the data set. Providing a trend and forecasting backwards was challenging and may have significantly biased the data. Looking at the information using the GDP data with the minimum, maximum, and average values included for the blank values, it was determined that there was less bias using the minimum GDP as it would least likely over estimate the wealth of the country. Using the minimum values appeared to produce the least bias in the data as it did not show the country to be more productive than it was at its minimum points.

    Additional data needed

  • Information on rates of cancer
  • Life expectancy for cancer patients
  • Availability of treatments for cancer
  • Data on population smoking percentage by gender
  • More refined population density data
  • More refined CO2 emissions density data
  • Access to affordable healthcare
  • Cultural norms surrounding healthcare


    • In the end it was determined that we would need to correlate the long term concerns of CO2 and cigarette smoking and correlate the average life expectancies per country to determine when the effect of the environmental factors were directly or indirectly correlated to the lung cancer death rates.
